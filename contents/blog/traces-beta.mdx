Traces is now in beta. If you've been using [PostHog Logs](/logs), you probably sensed this was coming.

PostHog starts with your user data and provides you with all the tools you need to build successful products. Debugging is one of the processes you definitely need to keep your products working as expected.

So far, we've had [Error tracking](/error-tracking), [Session replay](/session-replay), and [Logs](/logs) in the PostHog debugging stack, and Traces was a logical addition that will give you more of the context you need to resolve issues quickly.

## Why we built it this way (spoiler alert: not just another tracing tool)

Every observability tool we've used makes the same assumption: a trace is something an SRE looks at on a dashboard. Spans, services, latency, hosts. The user who hit the bug is somewhere else, in your analytics, your session replay, your customer support tool. This means you have to juggle all these tools to find the user context.

PostHog flips that (or should I use more SaaS-friendly talk and say we're DISRUPTING the market). We built observability on top of analytics, not the other way around. So when a span lands in PostHog, it lands in the same project as your replays, your errors, your logs, and the user who triggered it. Same identity, same session, same event stream. Every span has a face.

That shared foundation is live in the beta. The workflows that pay it off: click a span and watch the replay, pivot from a slow request to the customer it cost you, are what's next on the roadmap, not in the box today. We wanted to ship the honest version first.

## What's in the beta

Point your existing OpenTelemetry exporter at PostHog's OTLP/HTTP endpoint, drop in a bearer token, and you're sending traces. No new SDK, no agent on the box, no rewrite of the instrumentation you already have.

```bash
OTEL_EXPORTER_OTLP_TRACES_ENDPOINT="<your-posthog-host>/i/v1/traces"
OTEL_EXPORTER_OTLP_TRACES_HEADERS="Authorization=Bearer <your-project-api-key>"
OTEL_SERVICE_NAME="my-app"
```

That's the config change.

Inside PostHog, you get a volume sparkline for traffic at a glance, a filterable table of root spans (slice by service, name, kind, status, duration, trace or span ID, or any attribute on the span), and a flamegraph for any trace you click into. All of it lives on the same ClickHouse fleet as your PostHog Logs, so the same query layer runs both.

It's the boring, working version of distributed tracing, already sitting next to the rest of your PostHog data.

## Debugging with PostHog

Say a user complains your checkout is slow. Because their `user_id` is on both your OTel spans and your PostHog events, you can pull up every span they triggered in the last hour and find the four-second wait on a downstream payment provider. From there, opening their session replay in another tab shows exactly what they were staring at while the request hung: same person, same session, all in the project you're already in.

That's a workflow you can run on day one. What we haven't built yet is the connective tissue that makes it feel native: clicking a slow span and having the replay open beside it, or clicking an error and watching the trace it caused appear in line. The shared identity layer is what makes those jumps possible, and it's already there. The UI to skip the tab-juggling is next on the roadmap.

Once those links are wired, this is where PostHogAI starts to earn its keep, answering something like *"show me the slowest spans this week for users on the Pro plan who hit feature flag X"* with one prompt instead of cobbling together four queries across four tools.
